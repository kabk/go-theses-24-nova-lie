Shoeprints from the muddy path across the rain-touched, cracked pavement. Moss-filled crevices and the weeds reclaiming a forgotten corner. The crumbling wall with graffiti and ivy, and the efforts to remove them both. Shadows casted by the urban structures and the leaves of the tree. Broken windows and a puddle reflecting the landscape in a distorted mirrored dimension. This essay is a reflective exploration of Graphic Events, the visual proof of inevitable change, that is the real life of our designed graphics. The story unfolds to reveal signs of the twilight-zone where human creations meet nature reasserting its presence—returning to its home amid a capitalist-commercial visual monoculture. This study hopes to explore and deepen the understanding of the visual culture that is created as effects of human impact on the ecology of our planet. Through analysing and deconstructing the Graphic Event’s perceivable materiality I aim to find the sublime in the everyday so-called mundane graphics.

Introduction to Graphic Events

Shadows casted by our designs, as the ever-shifting dance of clouds and the sun light up the scene. Whether it’s the patterns of dust or snow settling on surfaces, the vibrant chaos of a changing graffiti-covered brickwall, or the gradual decay of a discarded piece of paper—These visual phenomena vividly reflect the dynamic life inherent in Graphic Events.

The term was first coined by James Dyer and Nick Deakin in their book “Graphic Events: A Realist Account of Graphic Design”1 published in 2022. Here, they stress the importance on distinguishing a design from its lived life, as we often treat a graphic as a fixed substance, “…denying its life and dismissing it’s vitality.”2 Talking with James Williams, they discuss the concept of ‘process philosophy’ that prioritises that events are in motion. On the importance on keeping things in motion and having an accurate representation on how things exist, he answers them, “There are two reasons. The first is that by dismissing or denying change you are committing a violent act; you are not only denying the thing that is changing you are also denying all the values and lives around it.”3 They refer to Alfred North Whitehead that describes the so-called “Event” as “wherever or whenever something is going on.”4 This means that everything from a moving atom at a metaphysical level, to a seemingly static light pole in the city is in a constant state of flux as they constantly interact with their environment as a dynamic entity, enduring its existence.

Graphic Events as a concept, encapsulates a diverse array of visuals, making it challenging to pin where one ends and another begins. The term embraces subjectivity, acknowledging that Graphic Events are inherently subjective and can only be fully perceived through the eyes of an observer at a specific moment in time. This inherent subjectivity underscores the uniqueness of each event, highlighting the complex and dynamic nature of this concept and why I find it important to document them.

At every moment in time there is change happening, whether a mountain erosion or the sun moving across the sky. Our designed materials undergo constant change, developing a life on their own. “This is how it is with objects. They are mostly mute about their journeys, though most of them have traveled much farther than any of us.»5 External forces and internal mechanisms inevitably lead to decay, guiding the matter through its life cycle. Graphic Events serve as a visual testament to the resilience of nature, showcasing the universal truth that all entities adhere to the same cosmic laws. This worldview opens an endless stream of unique motifs as the events manifests as nature’s visual response to our designs and inventions. They are the proof, or rather the silent witnesses observing the constant motion of the currently anthropocentric world.

Despite domination of our surroundings though altered cityscapes and human attempts to control and shape the environment as some sort of masters of the cosmos, the shapes of Nature are present. Either in powerful ways such as extreme weather, natural hazards, pandemics, and warzones. Even human-made environmental impacts like deforestation and contaminated soil as results of industrialization and climate change. Or in subtle ways, camouflaged as the weathered, the unkempt, and the signs of the past such as fingerprints, graffiti, and scratches, as well as Nature’s designs of a pile of leaves on the pavement. Everything is undergoing its own cycle of life and death to pave the way for new beginnings. This fundamental truth, rooted in the natural order, persists in the face of human inventions and interventions. This essay advocates for a design philosophy that collaborates and harmonizes with these events, rejecting thoughtlessness and disrespect on the importance of what these happenings can tell us about ourselves and our surroundings. Ultimately revealing the face of the unregulated visual testimony that is our non-designed visual culture.

As citizens of the Age of the Mechanical Reproduction,6 we can mass-produce items with perfectly smooth and consistent surfaces. Vectorized and photoshopped visuals are dominant in both our physical surroundings but also in our hyper engineered digital landscape. A standardized visual language has taken over both the web and the world. As we start to speak the same vocal language, we also begin to speak the same visual language. The sans-serif flat logo trend is popular because it prioritizes functionality and accessibility, the result is an aesthetic monoculture.7 The refreshing presence of Graphic Events becomes a contrast to this notion, as something new and unique.

Shapes of the Events

Graphic Events can possess a sublime aesthetic beauty that is worth taking notice of. Whether for creative expression or philosophic reflection–the cyclical weathered phenomena can serve as inspiration for its protesting and persistent qualities. In this chapter I will explore our shared, non-designed visual culture and the materiality of what they can look like through four patterns already found in nature: Meander, Packing, Cracks and Explosion. I am using these brackets not as strict separations but rather as organizational tools to comprehend and discuss their visual characteristics and their perceivable materiality. While mathematics may explain their creation, this essay takes a different path, as I am exploring them through a typology of my own eyes. As human survival is deeply rooted in recognizing patterns to anticipate and understand our surroundings, I believe that designers and creatives should gain a better vocabulary when talking about the dynamic interplay or forces and the inherent irregularity of organic processes inherent in Graphic Events. I hope this analysis together with the deconstruction of the events can help to overcome some of the stigma surrounding their presence and highlight the romantic opportunistic life of Graphic Events. With this effort of acknowledging their characteristics I aim to make encountering them more accessible to the average person to appreciate their aesthetic qualities.

Decay—Types of Weathering

(Sketch) In George Simmel's “Die Ruine” (1911) A crumbing ruin from what was once a human-made structure can be seen as nature transforming the materials she once served for artwork, into materials for her own artistic expression.8

“The balance between nature and spirit which the building manifested shifts in favour of nature. The shift becomes a cosmic tragedy. Which so we feel, makes every ruin infused with our nostalgia.: for now the decay appears as natures revenge. For the spirit's having violated it by making a form in it's own image.”9

Types of weathering:

According to David Suzuki there are three types of decay:
Mechanical—An outer factor or impact
Chemical—The chemical structure is altered. Because it is a universal solvent, water is an effective agent in the weathering ad decomposing rocks.
Biological—A natural process within the object.

Only two hundred years ago, it was though that mountains, lakes and deserts were permanent and unchanging. Now we know that the surface of the globe has been undergoing constant change since its beginning—and that it still is; even mountains can wear down over time though weathering. The smooth impeccable surface will be roughened; wear and tear will heave and crack the concrete, water draining from driveways beyond will erode channels. Imagine people feet wearing away the surface particle by particle, dust-laden winds scouring the surface, winter rain infiltrating a thousand tiny pores and expanding as it freezes.
Water dissolving, the sun staining or altering the ink. Corroding substances.

Endo decay— When decomposition comes from the inside
Exo decay— Where the decomposition is caused by an external environmental force.
The two processes are often entangled and hardly ever neat.

Geotrauma Condensation and the hydrologic cycle. In many ways you can look at everything as a transportation of water.
The whole enterprise of life as just a vehicle for the transformation of water. My dad was the one who first pointed out and introduced me to recognizing and appreciating the beauty of unplanned visual compositions. He has always had an obsession over unique and ideosyntactic textures found in the wild. Poster residue, water damage, dirt splatter, cracks, scratches, scuffs, graffiti, peeling paint, discoloration, rust stains, dents, and chips. Whenever he came across something that caught his eye, he would swiftly retrieve his camera from his belt bag. My recent study of the Flâneur, has led me to realize that he embodies this concept himself as a connoisseur of the streets. The term first came about in 19th-century Paris. Then referring to a man in the city wandering and observing, without being observed. My dad’s tendency to walk ten meters behind my mother and me, always on the lookout for an image, drifting off, disappearing into the visuals of the landscape—confirms my theory. I always appreciated his willingness to take his time to show me these compositions, non the less even taking the time to move closer to them to observe the details. But as I looked around me I was surprised not to find more people stopping to pay close attention to matters like him.
