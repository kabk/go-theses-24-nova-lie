In this essay, I am delving into a reflective exploration of “Graphic Events”—the visual manifestations of nature's second skin manifesting as decay, decomposition, ruins, or patina. Through analysing and deconstructing the graphic event’s perceivable materiality, I aim to find the sublime in the everyday visual ecology that surrounds us nowadays. The narrative reveals a world where torn posters, paths, cloud formations, cracks in the asphalt, graffiti and remnants of crumbling buildings are all signs of nature reasserting its presence, returning to its home. How then, can we cultivate an appreciation for these events and realign ourselves with the inevitable decay that seems to embody nature's response to human attempts at mastering the cosmos?

Introduction to Graphic Events

The ever-shifting dance of clouds, raindrops adorning a window casting a shadow, sunlight casting a glow on a poster, the dog-eared pages of a book, and the remnants of a peeled-off sticker—all encapsulate the transient and ephemeral beauty of everyday visuals. Whether it’s the patterns of snow or sand settling on surfaces, the vibrant chaos of a changing graffiti-covered wall, or the gradual decay of a discarded piece of paper on the ground, these visual phenomena vividly reflect the dynamic life inherent in “Graphic Events”.

We often treat a graphic as a fixed substance, “…denying its life and dismissing it’s vitality.”, 1 state James Dyer and Nick Deakin in their book “Graphic Events: A Realist Account of Graphic Design” published in 2022. Here, they coined the term ‘Graphic Events’, with a wish to distinguish real-life graphics from their design, highlighting “the dynamic conditions of graphics that evade design.” They refer to Alfred North Whitehead and his work “The Concept of Nature.” 2 According to Whitehead, “wherever or whenever something is going on there is an event.” This means that everything from a moving atom at a metaphysical level, to a light pole in the city is in a constant state of flux. Even seemingly static objects, such as a brick wall, are in ‘motion’ and constantly interacting with their environment as a dynamic entity, as it is continually undergoing change.

Throughout the ages, humans have sought to assert control and dominance over their natural surroundings, an inevitable narrative in the ongoing saga between humanity and nature. Whether manifested in extreme weather, weeds, the decay of materials— the overarching cycle of life and death, have underscored the inevitability of mortality, the fact that the universe is ever-changing. Simultaneously, human intervention has sought to assert dominance over the natural world, driven by the desire for control with a wish to immortalize our inventions and ideas. To control and maintain a specific visual desire. In the context of a capitalist society, where the pursuit of efficiency and perfection takes precedence to maximize profit, the normalization of visual monotony becomes understandable. The footprints of our human ancestors looks like a story of an imperialistic war against the cosmos, with us in one end trying to name and colonize the galaxy and on the other end, nature, with its quiet, persistent revenge. In George Simmel’s “Die Ruine” (1911) he puts the fascination of patina so beautifully:

“Nature has transformed the work of art into material for her own expression, as she had previously served as material for art.”
&
In the formation of patina, too, a natural process takes place on the surface of a human product and produces a growth of skin which completely covers up the original one. That the product becomes more beautiful by chemical and physical means; that what has been willed becomes, without intention or force, something obviously new, often more beautiful, and once more self. Consistent—this is the mysterious harmony which is the fantastic fascination of patina; and it cannot be wholly accounted for by analyzing our perception of it.”

Nature, in its role as a formidable force, resists these attempts, and manifestations of protest and resistance emerge as comprehensive graphic events. Despite concerted efforts to limit and control our surroundings, the cosmic tragedy prevails—everything must undergo the cycle of death to pave the way for new beginnings. This fundamental truth, rooted in the natural order, persists in the face of human inventions and interventions. The essay delves into the intricate dance between human aspirations for control and the resilient, regenerative inspirational forces of nature, ultimately revealing the face of the unregulated visual testimony that is our non-designed visual culture.

The concept of Graphic Events encapsulates a diverse array of visuals, making it challenging to pin where one ends and another begins. The term embraces subjectivity, acknowledging that Graphic Events are inherently subjective and can only be fully perceived through the eyes of an observer at a specific moment in time. This inherent subjectivity underscores the uniqueness of each event, highlighting the complex and dynamic nature of graphic phenomena. Graphic Events serve as a visual testament to the resilience of nature, showcasing the universal truth that all entities adhere to the same cosmic laws. The visual qualities of our carefully designed human objects transcend their original intent, developing a life on their own. External forces and internal mechanisms inevitably lead to decay, guiding the matter through its life cycle.
