Shoeprints from the muddy path across the rain-touched, cracked pavement. Moss-filled crevices and the weeds reclaiming a forgotten corner. The crumbling wall with graffiti and ivy, and the efforts to remove them both. The shadows casted by the urban structures and the leaves of the tree. Broken windows and puddles reflecting the landscape in a distorted mirrored dimension. This essay is a reflective exploration of Graphic Events, the visual proof of inevitable change, that is the real life of our designed graphics. The story unfolds to reveal signs of the twilight-zone where human creations meet nature reasserting its presence—returning to its home amid a capitalist-commercial visual monoculture. This study hopes to explore and deepen the understanding of the visual culture that is created as effects of human impact on the ecology of our planet. Through analysing and deconstructing the Graphic Event’s perceivable materiality I aim to find the sublime in the everyday so-called mundane graphics.

Introduction to Graphic Events

Shadows casted by our designs, as the ever-shifting dance of clouds and the sun light up the scene. Whether it’s the patterns of dust or snow settling on surfaces, the vibrant chaos of a changing graffiti-covered brickwall, or the gradual decay of a discarded piece of paper—These visual phenomena vividly reflect the dynamic life inherent in Graphic Events. The term was first coined by James Dyer and Nick Deakin in their book “Graphic Events: A Realist Account of Graphic Design”1 published in 2022. Here, they stress the importance on distinguishing a design from its lived life, as we often treat a graphic as a fixed substance, “…denying its life and dismissing it’s vitality”.2 Talking with James Williams, they discuss the concept of ‘process philosophy’ that prioritises that events are in motion. On the importance on keeping things in motion and having an accurate representation on how things exist, he answers, “There are two reasons. The first is that by missing of denying change you are committing a violent act; you are not only denying the thing that is changing you are also denying all the values and lives around it.”3 They also refer to Alfred North Whitehead that describes the so-called “Event” as “wherever or whenever something is going on there is an event.” This means that everything from a moving atom at a metaphysical level, to a seemingly static light pole in the city is in a constant state of flux as they constantly interact with their environment as a dynamic entity, enduring its existence.

In the formation of patina, too, a natural process takes place on the surface of a human product and produces a growth of skin which completely covers up the original one. That the product becomes more beautiful by chemical and physical means; that what has been willed becomes, without intention or force, something obviously new, often more beautiful, and once more self. Consistent—this is the mysterious harmony which is the fantastic fascination of patina; and it cannot be wholly accounted for by analyzing our perception of it.”
